Interactive Media Assignment 1- Sakura Phoparisut

Frank Gehry and the Walt Disney Concert Hall

What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

The bottom half of the initial screen, where it reads “Sculpting Harmony”. The text is in a bold font and covers half the screen. The letters move, shrinking and growing according to the position of the mouse. Furthermore, when the mouse is moved over this section, it leaves a trail of smaller white text of Frank Gehry’s comments on the Walt Disney Concert Hall. The animation and trailing text is what drew my eyes to this section of the screen.

Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

  • Toggling the sound on and off
  • Accessing the menu and skipping to specific sections
  • Scrolling to progress through the chapters
  • Clicking a button to explore a 3D model of different objects in the presented videos- for example, a model of a layout of the Concert Hall or of the pipe organ centrepiece
  • Clicking on specific parts of a model to get more information on that particular section
  • Clicking to read more text on different parts of the Concert Hall’s creation
  • Being able to pause, play and skip through videos
  • The option to switch to Spanish can be chosen from the menu
  • Clicking on the Getty button in the corner will take you back to the top

What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

All the sections with the option to look at the 3D models- these were the more interactive parts of the website, since you could move and rotate the models, as well as look at the notes that have been attached to them. They are also accessed in the video segments of the exhibit that they are connected to, so people are able to pause the video and look at the objects being talked about for themselves.

What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

The way this site presents information makes scrolling a more interactive action in comparison to regular sites. Because it is meant to be a linear experience, they make it more interactive by having moving parts that respond to scrolling in more visually interesting ways. This is done in order to give viewers the impression that they are progressing through an exhibit. For example, this site has sections where blocks of text move along and stretch and squish with the scrolling.

What is the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

The main purpose of this site is to promote and educate people about the Walt Disney Concert Hall. The information presented comes in the forms of text, videos and interactive models, all ordered to create a narrative about the creation of the Concert Hall in a manner similar to a museum exhibit.

How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

Scrolling and seeing how the text and background colours move by- making scrolling through a single linear page the main way of progressing just like on any other site means people will understand what to do, and adding an additional bit of interaction through giving people the ability to play with the 3D models and view the notes attached to them keeps them interested along the way. As the site garners interest in the Concert Hall, there may be people that want to review specific pieces of information from this site, made easier with a chapter select menu.

What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time?

This site is primarily designed to be visited one time. It is a long, linear experience with multiple videos and interactive segments, and does not get any updates or changes over time. It's possible to quit at certain points and easily go back to them, as long as you remember where you left off, but overall the main experience this site was made with in mind is a single run through the page.

How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

People are still able to revisit the site if they quit midway, or if they are someone like a student, need to review particular information. This was done by adding chapters and sectioning their videos to make it easier to navigate and skip through to specific areas. However, the site is still just meant to be one long interactive exhibit, so it does not save or track progress, and does not get updated, and so there is only a limited amount of information people can revisit this site for.

What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

The way this site is built is as an exhibition. Therefore there are things to look at with information attached, with an option for people to do further reading on the site if they felt like it, as well as the 3D models as interactive features. The written sections are in small paragraphs in wide open spaces exactly like a real museum. Exhibitions are set up with the user’s experience in mind, so the page is set up in a very linear way to help curate the experience and adds fitting background music that adds to the atmosphere.

What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

As an exhibit, it encourages people experiencing it for the first time to look through the whole thing in a straight line. There are no branching paths, but there are places to stay on- for example, sections with videos and models, or sections with a "read more" option. The index also provides extra images related to the creation of the Concert Hall for those who want to see more. People aren't forced to stick around for these sections, but they can if tehy want to, much like in a physical exhibition.

What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

This site is created to be an educational resource and is modelled based on real life museum exhibitions, so it aims to make people curious about the Concert Hall. The information is all delivered through interactive models, videos and blocks of text separated by giant headers and images, all of which help to retain the viewer's attention. Aside from visual design, they also use sound in the form of symphony music playing in the background to enhance the atmosphere of a refined concert hall.

What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

I found it visually satisfying to watch all the text animations, particularly for the chapter header sections and places where they use the giant bold font. As you scroll by, the large text stretches and squishes according to its position on the screen, similarly to the title of the site. Chapter headers have the text arranged to match their respective subjects and have pen scribbles in the background that respond to the movement of your mouse. Clicking on the menu also has this animation where they bring in giant text saying "Chapters", which gets squished down by the clickable chapter items. The font and animation styles go really well together and makes the page feel a bit more fun overall, which is why I like it so much.
